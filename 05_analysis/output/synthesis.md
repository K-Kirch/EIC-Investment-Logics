## 2. Key Analytical Findings

### 2.1 Logic Succession Across the Innovation Pipeline

The most structurally consistent finding across the readthrough: as TRL increases, the dominant institutional logic shifts from `LOGIC_SCIENCE` to `LOGIC_MARKET` to `LOGIC_STATE`.

- **Pathfinder (TRL 1–4):** `LOGIC_SCIENCE` dominates the Specific Objectives of all three Challenge chapters. Scientific methodology, capability advancement, and researcher-led agenda are the organising frames.
- **Pathfinder Challenges — Expected Outcomes/Impacts:** `LOGIC_STATE` increasingly organises as outputs move toward societal and sovereignty goals.
- **STEP Scale Up (commercialisation-ready):** `LOGIC_STATE` dominates the instrument architecture; `LOGIC_MARKET` is genuinely operative in the award criteria (Table 9 Impact section) but is mediated by state selection mechanisms throughout.

This succession is not a smooth transition — it is punctuated by embedded market requirements in science-phase Challenges ([102], [109]) and by science criteria in the STEP award criteria ([169]). These cross-logic embeddings are among the most analytically productive findings for the inductive pass.

### 2.2 Tension-Dissolution as Institutional Practice (Pattern A)

Across the Overview, Pathfinder Open evaluation criteria, and Challenge Expected Impacts, logics are routinely presented as naturally complementary without acknowledging conflict. Key instances:

- **[082]:** State-defined Challenge relevance embedded as the primary sub-criterion of scientific excellence — the most consequential instance, shaping how applicants must frame their science to receive funding.
- **[057]:** Market success and societal impact presented as equivalent and complementary dimensions of the Impact criterion.
- **[102]:** "Potential markets and associated impacts" appended to science objectives via "also" — subordinate but mandatory.

Tension-dissolution is not neutral — it is a legitimacy move. Presenting logics as complementary performs institutional coherence rather than arguing for it, suppressing acknowledgement of genuine conflicts (e.g., open science vs. IP exploitation; scientific curiosity vs. market readiness).

### 2.3 TRL as Institutional Disguise

The TRL framework presents what is effectively a regulatory gatekeeping instrument as a scientific measurement tool. TRL determines eligibility, budget allocation, and instrument routing — but is framed as an objective developmental metric. This depoliticises what are political decisions about where in the innovation pipeline public money flows. The framework borrowed from NASA/US defence contexts carries legitimacy as scientific vocabulary while performing state regulatory functions. See [012], [044], [046].

### 2.4 Programme Manager: Soft Coercive Authority (Pattern B)

A progressive escalation of Programme Manager authority across [021]–[027] and [061]:

- Formal autonomy protections stated ([021]: "Awardees will bear no obligation")
- Milestone reviews overseen by Programme Managers ([023])
- Suspension/termination authority for lost relevance ([024])
- Projects "may be requested to join" thematic portfolios ([025])
- Programme Manager establishes common roadmap and proactively steers portfolio ([026])
- Dual responsiveness mandate (scientific AND industrial/market developments) without resolution mechanism ([061])

A hired technocrat, not a peer scientist or elected official, holds decisive authority over capital continuity. The gap between stated autonomy and operative control is structurally significant. Whether this constitutes a distinct administrative-managerial logic or `LOGIC_STATE` operating through professional instruments is a key question for Pass 2.

### 2.5 Mimetic Isomorphism: DARPA and Temasek

The EU explicitly benchmarks STEP against US (DARPA) and Asian (Temasek, Chinese Government Guidance Funds) models. This is mimetic isomorphism (DiMaggio & Powell 1983) operating at the instrument design level: the EU adopts the structural features of other innovation finance models to acquire their legitimacy. The adoption is selective — the EU reproduces the equity/catalytic investment model while embedding it in EU procurement architecture (application portal, quarterly batches, Commission Award Decision). The result is a hybrid that does not fully replicate the models it cites.

### 2.6 STEP Scale Up: State Absorbing Market Vocabulary

A sub-pattern running through the entire STEP section (identified at [130], developed across [132]–[180]):

> `LOGIC_STATE` absorbs and deploys market vocabulary (market gap, Venture Debt, equity, deal flow) as its own legitimation register. The market vocabulary does not signal market logic governing the passage — it signals the state justifying its intervention through market failure language.

This is distinct from Pattern A (logics presented as complementary). Here, the state is not acknowledging a second logic — it is using market vocabulary as the idiom in which state logic articulates itself. Per Rule 15 (vocabulary does not determine logic), each instance was tested individually. The sub-pattern holds across: market failure rationale ([130], [146]), InvestEU complementarity ([146]), investment mechanics ([132]), criticality conditions ([139]).

Exceptions — where `LOGIC_MARKET` is genuinely operative:
- Table 9 market opportunity criterion ([172])
- Table 9 business model criterion ([173])
- Table 9 innovation criterion (cost/performance dimension) ([169])

These are the passages where market logic actually governs the evaluative frame.

### 2.7 STEP Scale Up: Structural Mandate for Circular Market Validation

The most significant analytical finding in the STEP section ([140], [141], [176]):

STEP simultaneously requires:
1. **Demonstrated market interest** — pre-commitment from a qualified investor (20% of target round, [141])
2. **Demonstrated market failure** — the amount cannot be fully financed by private investors without EIC ([176])

These two requirements are only simultaneously satisfiable by using EIC's prospective 20–33% anchor participation as the lever to obtain the private pre-commitment. The private investor commits *because* EIC will co-invest; the EIC requires the commitment as proof of market interest. The market signal the state requires is constructed by the state's own potential participation.

Furthermore, the pre-commitment is explicitly non-legally binding ([141], footnote 177), and EIC holds 20–33% of the total round — a substantial anchor that materially conditions private co-investors' decisions. At this scale, private investors are not independently validating the company; they are co-investing around a dominant state anchor.

**Theoretical implication:** Whether this represents a fundamental contradiction (state claims to test market readiness while eliminating independent market assessment) or a deliberate developmental state design (state constructs market conditions it then deploys as legitimation) is the central theoretical question for the STEP analysis. The latter reading aligns with Mazzucato's mission-oriented investment model and Chinese Government Guidance Fund structures rather than liberal market VC logic.

### 2.8 The Sovereignty Seal as Field-Bridging Legitimacy Artefact

The Sovereignty (STEP) Seal is awarded by the state upon meeting evaluation thresholds and functions as a portable legitimacy credential that:

- Unlocks access to InvestEU Venture Debt ([152], [161])
- Enables access to Business Acceleration Services
- Can be awarded even on NO GO decisions (where merit criteria are met but budget is unavailable, [166])

The decoupling of the Seal from the investment decision ([166]) reveals it as a pure legitimacy instrument — the state certifying strategic value independently of whether it commits capital. The Seal travels across institutional fields (public EU programmes, private investors) carrying state-issued legitimacy. This is the state performing field-bridging through a portable artefact.

### 2.9 STEP: Hybrid Instrument — Market Tools, State Access Architecture

STEP deploys private equity mechanics (equity-only investment, case-by-case negotiation, co-investment, KYC screening) but accesses them through state tender infrastructure (Funding and Tenders Portal, quarterly evaluation batches, Commission Award Decision, jury process, 50-page business plan requirement). Neither logic fully governs the instrument as a whole; both are operative in different dimensions. Key markers:

| Market mechanics | State access architecture |
|---|---|
| Equity-only (no grant) | Quarterly evaluation batches |
| Case-by-case investment negotiation | Commission Award Decision |
| KYC/AML investor screening | Jury of maximum six members |
| 3–5x leverage requirement | 50-page business plan + FTO analysis |
| Investment Committee decision | Funding and Tenders Portal submission |

### 2.10 Sustainability Entry Mechanisms

Testing the flag raised in [096] — whether sustainability enters Challenge chapters as a standardised state logic insert or incidentally — revealed at least three distinct entry mechanisms:

1. **Commission Communication citation** ([099], II.2.1): sustainability enters structurally through the Commission Communication the Challenge cites (RePowerEU, Green Deal). Not rhetorical — the mandate flows from the cited instrument.
2. **Technical efficiency criterion as proxy** ([125], [129], II.2.3): no explicit sustainability language, but compute constraint requirements ("trained and deployed with constrained computational resources") may function as an indirect environmental mandate in the context of AI data centre energy use.
3. **Sustainability as sector definition** ([137], VI. STEP): the entire clean technology category is defined by sustainability criteria — sustainability is the technology category, not a frame applied to it.

II.2.3 (DeepRAP) has no sustainability language in Background/Scope or Expected Impact — supporting the hypothesis that sustainability enters through Commission Communications specific to the Challenge topic, not as a universal insert.

### 2.11 State Social Equity Template (Pattern C)

Near-identical passages embedding gender balance, female researcher empowerment, and underrepresented groups appear across multiple sections: [001], [017], [047], [063], [112], [175]. The recurrence and textual similarity suggest standardised compliance text originating at EU legislative level (Horizon Europe Regulation gender mainstreaming obligations) rather than programmatic commitments unique to the EIC. The tiebreaker paragraph ([059]) confirms gender balance as a structurally embedded state priority in the evaluation architecture.

## 5. Theoretical Implications (Provisional)

**For Scott's three pillars:** The EIC instruments operate primarily through the regulative pillar (legal mandates, eligibility rules, coercive authority) with normative pillar elements (professional standards, FAIR principles, KYC/AML norms) and limited cognitive pillar presence (TRL as taken-for-granted metric, market failure as unargued premise).

**For DiMaggio & Powell isomorphism:** Mimetic isomorphism is explicit (DARPA, Temasek benchmarking). Coercive isomorphism operates through the Horizon Europe Regulation, EU AI Act, Chips Act citations. Normative isomorphism operates through professional expert evaluation, investment advisers, and TDD requirements.

**For Thornton et al. institutional logics:** The four logics are all present but differentially distributed. Critically, `LOGIC_MARKET` is genuinely operative only in specific passages (Table 9 Impact criteria, IP strategy criterion) while appearing pervasively as a vocabulary register for `LOGIC_STATE`. This distinction — market logic as register vs. market logic as organising frame — is the most analytically productive refinement produced in Pass 1.

**For Mazzucato's mission-oriented investment:** STEP's circular market validation design, state anchor position (20–33%), and Sovereignty Seal as field-bridging artefact are more consistent with Mazzucato's patient capital / mission-oriented investor model than with VC or conventional state aid logic. The instrument claims liberal market mechanics while operating through developmental state design principles.

**For Suchman's legitimacy theory:** The Sovereignty Seal is a pragmatic legitimacy device (gives access to resources), with moral legitimacy undertones (strategic alignment with EU interests). The tension-dissolution pattern (Pattern A) is a cognitive legitimacy strategy — presenting the instrument as coherent and internally consistent rather than arguing for it.
