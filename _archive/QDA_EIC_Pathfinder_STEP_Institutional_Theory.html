Qualitative Data Analysis: EIC Pathfinder & STEP

Lens: Institutional Theory  |  Source: EIC Work Programme 2026

Analytical Framework

This analysis applies Scott's Three-Pillar Model (1995/2014) as the primary organising structure, supplemented by:

  • DiMaggio & Powell's (1983) Isomorphic Mechanisms (coercive, mimetic, normative)
  • Institutional Logics (Friedland & Alford 1991; Thornton et al. 2012): State, Market, Science/Profession
  • Legitimacy Theory (Suchman 1995): pragmatic, moral, cognitive

The two programmes — EIC Pathfinder (early-stage deep tech research) and EIC STEP Scale Up (strategic technology commercialisation) — are analysed both independently and comparatively to reveal how the EIC as an institutional actor constructs, mediates, and enforces institutional rules across different stages of the innovation pipeline.

1. Regulative Pillar

Rules, laws, monitoring, sanctions; compliance yields legitimacy

1.1 EIC Pathfinder

Evidence (verbatim)Analysis
"support for the earliest stages of scientific, technological or deep tech research and development" Regulatory boundary-setting: defines the jurisdictional scope of the programme and excludes later-stage work
"Technology Readiness Levels from 1 to 4" (TRL framework) Codification of a regulative standard borrowed from aerospace/defence (NASA origin); functions as a compliance threshold disguised as a scientific framework
"EIC Pathfinder Challenges… within predefined thematic areas" Top-down regulatory channelling of research; the state pre-selects themes (Advanced Materials, Healthy Ageing, Cognitive AI), constraining academic freedom under a regulative logic
Eligibility: consortium or single entity; admissibility page limits Bureaucratic compliance architecture; formal rules create barriers to entry and screen out non-conformant applicants
Annex 6 – Additional IP provisions for Pathfinder Regulative control over intellectual property generated with public funds; signals ownership expectations even in early-stage science
Key Observation: The Pathfinder operationalises state regulation of science through a TRL taxonomy that appears cognitive (a shared technical schema) but functions regulatively (as a gatekeeping mechanism). This is a form of institutional disguise — regulatory control presented as scientific neutrality.

1.2 EIC STEP Scale Up

Evidence (verbatim)Analysis
"following the STEP Regulation (EU) 2024/795" Explicit legislative origin; STEP derives its authority from a supranational regulatory act, making coercive isomorphism the foundational institutional mechanism
"KYC by the EIC Fund or the EIF" (Know Your Customer / Anti-Money Laundering) Financial sector regulation imported into the innovation funding domain; extends state surveillance into investor-company relationships
"Concurrent submissions to both the EIC Accelerator and EIC STEP Scale Up calls are not permitted" Anti-gaming rule; regulative enforcement of programme boundaries
"After three unsuccessful submissions… an applicant may not apply again to the EIC STEP Scale Up call under the Horizon Europe Framework Programme" Permanent exclusion mechanism — an unusually punitive regulative instrument for an innovation programme; signals the scarcity and strategic gravity of the call
"The EIC Fund will ensure that supported companies keep most of their value, including their IP, in the EU" Extraterritorial IP retention clause; extends EU regulatory reach into private investment decisions post-award; analogous to national security review mechanisms (e.g., CFIUS)
"specific safeguards may be introduced in the investment agreement… to protect European interests" Open-ended regulative intervention clause; grants the EIC Fund discretionary power to impose conditions, constituting a coercive mechanism
Key Observation: STEP is the most explicitly regulative programme in the EIC portfolio. Its architecture mirrors financial market regulation (KYC, investment due diligence, equity-only instruments) more than traditional research funding. The IP retention clause represents a significant extension of state regulatory authority into private company governance — a form of regulatory reach-back with geopolitical motivation.

2. Normative Pillar

Values, norms, roles, obligations; conformity yields appropriateness

2.1 EIC Pathfinder

Evidence (verbatim)Analysis
"Do you have an ambitious vision for a novel future technology that could make a real difference to our lives?" Normative interpellation of the ideal applicant — the visionary scientist-entrepreneur; constructs a role identity that fuses academic and entrepreneurial norms
"realise an ambitious vision for radically new technology, with potential to create new markets, respond to societal needs and/or to provide solutions for global challenges" Normative hierarchy: scientific ambition is legitimised through market creation AND societal contribution — both market and civic logics are invoked as normative obligations
"interdisciplinary team of researchers and innovators" Normative valorisation of collaboration across disciplinary boundaries; signals that siloed expertise is insufficient for EIC legitimacy
"EIC Pathfinder Open" vs. "EIC Pathfinder Challenges" Normative tension: Open valorises scientific autonomy and researcher-led inquiry; Challenges imposes state-directed norms onto the research agenda. The co-existence of both signals a negotiated normative order
"validate the scientific basis… realise a proof of principle… explore paths to impact" Normative sequencing of research as purposive and commercialisation-oriented from the outset; redefines what counts as legitimate science
Key Observation: Pathfinder constructs a hybrid normative identity for participants: you must be a rigorous scientist and a market visionary and a collaborative team player. This triple normative obligation reflects an institutional logic blend — science, market, and civic logics are merged into a single expected role. Failure to perform any dimension risks non-compliance with the programme's normative expectations.

2.2 EIC STEP Scale Up

Evidence (verbatim)Analysis
"game-changing innovative technology" Normative framing of the ideal company as a disruptor; constructs a heroic entrepreneur role analogous to Silicon Valley cultural norms — mimetic import of US tech sector identity
"Does the successful commercialisation… represent significant risks which mean that it cannot be sufficiently financed from the market investors?" Market failure narrative normalises state investment; the normative expectation is that private markets should fund innovation, with state intervention positioned as an exception — not the norm
"propelling Europe's economic, industrial, and technological competitiveness" Civic/normative obligation framing: companies are expected not just to succeed commercially but to serve European strategic interests — a form of normative conscription
"adequate gender balance, with a credible plan to fill the gaps" Normative diversity obligation embedded in an evaluation criterion; signals that gender equity is now an institutional norm within the European innovation field, not merely aspirational
"Sovereignty (STEP) Seal" awarded even to NO GO proposals that meet criteria Decoupling of legitimacy from funding — the Seal functions as a normative certificate of alignment with EU strategic values, usable with other funders. This is a powerful institutional device: it extends EIC's normative influence beyond its own budget
"smart money" (referenced in BAS section) Normative reframing of public investment; positions the EIC not just as a funder but as a strategic partner — constructs a normative expectation of ongoing engagement, not a transactional grant
Key Observation: The Sovereignty Seal is the most institutionally novel device in the entire Work Programme. By awarding legitimacy independently of funding, STEP creates a meta-institutional instrument: the EIC certifies companies as normatively aligned with European strategic interests, enabling those companies to access other institutional fields (InvestEU, other EU programmes, national programmes). This is institutional field-bridging through a legitimacy artefact.

3. Cognitive Pillar

Shared beliefs, schemas, taken-for-granted assumptions; conformity yields meaning

3.1 EIC Pathfinder

Evidence (verbatim)Analysis
"deep tech" (repeated throughout) A taken-for-granted cognitive category in the European innovation field; the EIC treats "deep tech" as self-evident but never formally defines it in this section — it functions as a cognitive shorthand that signals shared assumptions among insiders
"breakthrough technologies" Schumpeterian creative destruction schema imported as a cognitive baseline; positions incremental innovation as institutionally less legitimate
"disrupt a field and a market or create new opportunities" Disruption as cognitive norm; the language of disruption (derived from Christensen's innovation theory) is naturalised as the expected outcome of Pathfinder research
"scientific basis to underpin breakthrough technologies" Cognitive sequencing assumption: science precedes technology precedes market. This linear model of innovation (science → technology → application) is taken for granted, despite decades of evidence for non-linear innovation pathways
TRL 1–4 as the Pathfinder domain Cognitive schema that maps innovation onto a linear developmental trajectory; the TRL system is treated as natural and objective but is in fact a socially constructed classification system with institutional origins in US defence procurement
Key Observation: Pathfinder's cognitive pillar rests on a linear innovation model that is treated as self-evident. The TRL schema, the disruption discourse, and the science-first sequencing all reflect taken-for-granted assumptions that shape what kinds of projects can even be imagined as Pathfinder-eligible. This cognitive infrastructure silently excludes innovation models that do not fit (e.g., user-led, practice-based, or incremental innovation).

3.2 EIC STEP Scale Up

Evidence (verbatim)Analysis
"strategic dependencies of the Union" Geopolitical vulnerability as a cognitive frame; takes for granted that Europe is dependent and exposed — a shared threat perception that motivates programme design
"market gap in financing deep tech scale up companies in Europe" Market failure as cognitive baseline; the assumption that private markets fail to fund European deep tech at scale is presented as an objective fact rather than a contested claim
"to match and ultimately surpass the performance of the USA and Asia" (from Strategic Goals) Competitive cognitive schema: the US and Asia are constructed as the reference group (mimetic target) against which European performance is measured; the gap is naturalised as a problem requiring institutional response
"semiconductor technologies and quantum technologies" (named as 2026 priorities) Cognitive crystallisation of technology categories as strategic; the naming of specific technologies signals that the EIC Board's cognitive map of what matters has become institutionalised
"Sovereignty (STEP) Seal" Sovereign independence as a cognitive ideal; the Seal makes the concept of "sovereignty" tangible and tradeable — it converts an abstract geopolitical aspiration into a certifiable institutional category
Equity-only (no grant) for STEP Cognitive assumption that market mechanisms (equity investment) are more appropriate for scale-up than grants; reflects financialisation as a taken-for-granted organising logic for later-stage innovation support
Key Observation: STEP's cognitive architecture is dominated by a geopolitical threat schema — strategic dependency, sovereignty, and competitive catch-up with the US/Asia. This schema is so embedded that it functions as a cognitive pre-condition for the programme's existence: without the shared belief that Europe is vulnerable and falling behind, STEP would lose its cognitive legitimacy.

4. Institutional Logics

Competing and co-existing logics operating within and across the programmes

LogicManifestation in PathfinderManifestation in STEP

Market Logic Secondary: "create new markets", commercialisation pathway, impact criteria Primary: equity investment, funding rounds, market opportunity assessment, "first come, first served"
State/Political Logic Tertiary: Challenges are state-directed themes; eligibility rules; IP provisions Primary: STEP Regulation origin, strategic dependency framing, sovereignty seal, IP retention in EU
Professional Logic Evaluation by experts (peer review model); EIC Jury Jury of "leading experts"; investment adviser; Technology Due Diligence by external expert

Logic Tensions and Negotiations

  1. Science vs. Market (Pathfinder): The Pathfinder Open call accommodates science logic (researcher-defined questions, TRL 1-4 scope), while Challenges subordinates science logic to state/market priorities (predefined themes, specific objectives). This creates an intra-programme logic tension that the Work Programme resolves through institutional segmentation (Open vs. Challenges).
  2. Market vs. State (STEP): STEP explicitly invokes market failure to justify state intervention, but the programme's design is more regulatory than market-mimicking. The equity-only instrument borrows market logic form while pursuing state logic goals. This is a case of logic borrowing — using market instruments to achieve geopolitical objectives.
  3. Science vs. State (across programmes): Pathfinder is science-logic dominant; STEP is state-logic dominant. The EIC portfolio as a whole reflects a logic succession model — as innovation moves from early-stage research to commercial scale-up, science logic gives way to market logic, which at the strategic scale-up level gives way to state logic. This is institutionally significant: it suggests that for the EIC, strategic importance ultimately trumps scientific merit.

5. Isomorphic Mechanisms

How the programmes produce and enforce conformity within the European innovation field

5.1 Coercive Isomorphism

  • STEP Regulation (EU) 2024/795 mandates the STEP Scale Up call's existence and scope; participating companies must conform to its technology classification or are excluded
  • IP retention clause: companies cannot relocate IP outside EU post-award without triggering safeguards — a coercive constraint on organisational behaviour
  • Three-strike exclusion rule (STEP): permanent exclusion from the programme after three failed attempts is an unusually strong coercive pressure
  • KYC/AML requirements apply financial sector regulation coercively to innovation companies and their investors
  • Chips Act alignment (STEP 2026 priorities for semiconductors and quantum): the Work Programme operationalises legislative priorities from the European Chips Act, demonstrating how one EU regulation coercively shapes another institution's priorities

5.2 Mimetic Isomorphism

  • "To match and ultimately surpass the performance of the USA and Asia" (Strategic Goal 4): the EIC explicitly models itself against US and Asian innovation systems; mimetic pressure from DARPA (US), ARPA-E, and Temasek (Singapore) is visible in the programme design
  • Unicorn target: the unicorn concept is a US venture capital metric imported into EU policy — a mimetic artefact
  • "Smart money" and Business Acceleration Services: mimics the value-add model of top-tier VC firms (Sequoia, a16z), which provide operational support alongside capital
  • Equity-only STEP instrument: mimics private venture/growth equity investment structures rather than traditional EU grant mechanisms

5.3 Normative Isomorphism

  • Jury evaluation model: importing professional norms from venture capital (pitch + expert jury = investment decision) into public funding
  • Gender balance requirement: diffusion of DEI professional norms across the organisational field through mandatory evaluation criterion
  • Business plan + pitch deck requirements (STEP): standardises the language and format of innovation presentations according to VC industry norms — organisations that cannot speak this language are excluded
  • "Freedom to Operate" (FTO) analysis requirement: IP profession's standard tool becomes a mandatory submission element, spreading IP professional norms across the applicant population

The Sovereignty Seal as a Legitimacy Innovation

The STEP Sovereignty Seal merits dedicated analysis as an institutional design innovation. It:

  1. Decouples legitimacy from resource allocation — companies receive the Seal even when rejected for funding, enabling them to access other institutional fields
  2. Creates a new institutional category — "sovereignty-aligned company" is a novel identity that did not exist before STEP
  3. Functions as a meta-institutional bridge — carries EIC's moral legitimacy into InvestEU, national programmes, and private investor relationships
  4. Quantifies strategic alignment — converts a qualitative assessment (contribution to European strategic interests) into a binary, portable credential

This represents a sophisticated understanding of how legitimacy operates as a resource in institutional fields (cf. Suchman 1995) — and it is arguably more institutionally durable than any single funding award.

Key Theoretical Contribution Points

  1. Logic succession across the innovation pipeline: The EIC programmes collectively demonstrate that as innovation moves from concept (Pathfinder TRL 1-4) to commercial scale (STEP EUR 50-150M rounds), the dominant institutional logic shifts from Science → Market → State. This is theoretically significant because it inverts the conventional assumption that state logic precedes market logic — here, the state re-enters at the highest stage of commercial development specifically to address geopolitical concerns.
  2. Institutional bricolage in programme design: Both programmes combine elements from multiple institutional logics and fields (academic peer review, VC investment, financial regulation, geopolitical policy). This bricolage is not incoherent — it is a deliberate strategy for constructing programmes that can claim legitimacy across multiple audiences simultaneously (researchers, investors, regulators, geopolitical stakeholders).
  3. The Sovereignty Seal as institutional innovation: STEP's introduction of a portable legitimacy credential that survives funding rejection is a novel institutional design. It extends the EIC's institutional field boundary beyond the boundaries of its own budget, constituting a form of institutional imperialism — the EIC shapes behaviour in other institutional fields (national programmes, private markets) through a legitimacy artefact.
  4. Cognitive infrastructure and exclusion: Both programmes rely on taken-for-granted schemas (TRL, disruption, strategic dependency, market gap) that simultaneously constitute the programme's internal coherence and exclude alternative innovation models. A Gramscian or critical institutionalist reading would flag this as hegemonic cognitive infrastructure — the rules of what counts as legitimate innovation are established by those who also control the funding.
